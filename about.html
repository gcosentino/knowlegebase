About

I'm Guilherme Raul Cosentino Ferreira. I test autonomous vehicle perception systems at NVIDIA. Before that I spent three years at Stellantis building simulation pipelines for ADAS and writing HVAC embedded software. I started an MS in Computer Science specializing in AI at Georgia Tech, but paused it to join NVIDIA.

I grew up in Recife, Brazil. I live in Sunnyvale, California.

The question I keep coming back to is deceptively simple: what does it mean to understand something? I test perception systems that classify objects in real time — the machine says "that's a pedestrian" and sometimes it's right and sometimes it's a fire hydrant. The system processes the scene. But it doesn't understand the scene. Where exactly is the line? I'm not sure anyone has a good answer.

That question pulls me in a lot of directions. Linguistics and natural language processing — how language carries meaning, and whether statistical models that fluently manipulate language are doing anything like what a brain does. Information theory and measurement theory — how you even begin to quantify something as slippery as consciousness or intelligence, and whether the act of measuring it changes what it is. Critical consciousness — the idea, rooted in the work of a philosopher who also grew up in Recife, that real understanding requires becoming aware of the systems you're embedded in, not just operating within them. A system that can't reflect on its own process — person, institution, or machine — is receiving without understanding.

And then there's the deeper version of the question. We don't know the exact moment chemistry becomes biology. Somewhere between self-replicating RNA molecules and the first cell, matter started caring about its own continuation. That transition — from stuff that happens to stuff that matters to the stuff it's happening to — is the origin of cognition, and we still can't explain it. If you think of living things as biological machines that reproduce themselves, we're self-replicating code running on wet hardware. Theoretically you could restructure other matter to perform the same functions. That sounds like science fiction, except we already have models that outperform most humans at specific cognitive tasks. The line between "processes information" and "understands" keeps moving. I'm trying to locate it.
