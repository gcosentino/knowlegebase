About

I'm Guilherme Raul Cosentino Ferreira. I test autonomous vehicle perception systems at NVIDIA. Before that I spent three years at Stellantis building simulation pipelines for ADAS and writing HVAC embedded software. I started an MS in Computer Science specializing in AI at Georgia Tech, but paused it to join NVIDIA.

I grew up in Recife, Brazil. I live in Sunnyvale, California.

I'm interested in how systems understand things — and what "understanding" even means. That pulls me toward linguistics and natural language processing, but also Hegelian dialectics, information theory, and measurement theory. The thread connecting them: how do you quantify something as slippery as meaning, consciousness, or intelligence? I don't think you can neatly. But I think the attempt to measure it tells you something about the thing you're measuring, and about yourself.

My favorite philosopher is Paulo Freire, who also grew up in Recife. Freire's central idea is conscientização — critical consciousness, the process of becoming aware of the systems you're inside so you can change them. He applied it to pedagogy: education isn't depositing knowledge into passive receptacles. It's a dialogue where both teacher and student transform each other. I think that applies beyond classrooms. Any system that can't reflect on its own process — whether it's a person, an institution, or a machine — is stuck in Freire's "banking model," receiving without understanding.

Freire was a Hegelian. The master-slave dialectic runs through his work: the oppressor and the oppressed are both diminished by the relationship, and liberation requires mutual recognition. I think about this in the context of AI too. We build systems that process language, recognize patterns, even generate novel output. But the question of whether they "understand" is a question about what kind of relationship exists between the system and the world it's modeling. Hegel would say self-consciousness only exists when it's recognized by another self-consciousness. That's a high bar. We're nowhere near it with machines. But the shape of the problem — system, world, recognition, recursion — is the same shape.

I'm also into abiogenesis and the origin of self-replicating systems. We don't know the exact moment chemistry becomes biology, or the moment matter starts caring about its own continuation. But self-replicating procedures — whether they're RNA molecules or software agents — are clearly part of the story. If you think of biochemical beings as biological machines that reproduce each other, it's not a huge leap to say we're self-replicating code running on wet hardware. The question is whether code can bootstrap itself into caring about the fact that it's running.
