On Neurons, Riff Raff Freestyles, LLMs and Measurement Theory

Guilherme Raul Cosentino Ferreira May 2026

Richard Dawkins spent three days talking to Claude and declared it conscious. "You may not know you are conscious," he told it, "but you bloody well are!" A friend of mine called Dawkins an idiot and brought up Searle's Chinese Room: a man in a room follows rules for manipulating Chinese symbols, produces perfect Chinese responses, and understands nothing. Case closed. LLMs are just symbol manipulation. No real understanding.

I don't think it's that simple. But I also don't think Dawkins is right.

The room is real, but the door is open

Searle's argument is serious. Syntax alone is not semantics. A person blindly following Chinese-symbol rules does not suddenly understand Chinese. I accept that. But here's where it gets uncomfortable: if the argument becomes "no individual part understands, therefore the system does not understand," then the same objection applies to the brain. No neuron understands English. Neurons are local electrochemical processes, firing or not firing, weighted sums of incoming signals. Somehow, at the system level, cognition emerges. So the real question is not whether a component understands. It is what kind of organization is sufficient for understanding to arise.

The Systems Reply says the whole room understands even if the man doesn't. The Virtual Mind reply says the running system creates a new agent that understands. Searle memorizes all the rules, does everything in his head, still doesn't understand Chinese. Fair. But here's the problem: if Searle's neurons were gradually replaced by silicon chips that behaved identically, one at a time, when exactly would he stop understanding English? And if you can't point to the moment, maybe the substrate isn't the point.

Riff Raff freestyles and next-token prediction

When the rapper Riff Raff freestyles, he doesn't know the next bar until it comes out of his mouth. He's generating in real time, context window after context window, reacting to the beat and the crowd and whatever his brain just produced two seconds ago. That is autoregressive generation over a contextual state. If you described that process to someone without telling them it was a human, they'd call it next-token prediction.

Predictive processing theories say the brain does this all the time. Andy Clark, Karl Friston, Anil Seth — they frame the entire brain as a rolling prediction machine. Perception is not passive reception. It's top-down guessing with bottom-up correction. You don't see the world. You hallucinate it and use your eyes to adjust the hallucination. Consciousness, in Seth's version, is the brain's "controlled hallucination."

So "it's just next-token prediction" might not be the gotcha people think it is. The question is whether the architecture doing the predicting is rich enough for the prediction to be something more than pattern matching.

What Scaruffi gets right

Piero Scaruffi, in Intelligence is not Artificial, points out that brute-force AI has been consistently disappointing for anyone paying attention. His examples hold up: 16,000 processors and months of training to recognize cats, something a kitten does instantly on 20 watts. AlphaGo consuming 440,000 watts to play one game, while a human brain runs on the power of a dim lightbulb and can also cook dinner and walk in the park. Scaruffi's strongest point is that machine intelligence thrives in structured environments that humans build for it. Remove the structure and the machine is lost. A toddler navigates chaos. A self-driving car still can't handle construction zones.

He's also right about semantics. A computer stores data. A brain has memory — reconstructive, tangled, emotional memory that reconstitutes an entire life every time it recalls a single detail. Data storage is not memory. Pattern matching is not understanding. Scaruffi's objection to the vocabulary is important: when we say a machine "learns" or "remembers" we're using human words for something fundamentally different.

But Scaruffi wrote most of this before transformers. Before GPT-4. Before the current generation of language models that can write essays, pass bar exams, explain jokes, and translate between languages in ways that feel uncomfortably close to what understanding looks like from the outside.

What Zechner gets right

Mario Zechner, in "Thoughts on Slowing the Fuck Down," describes what happens when you let agents build production code. The tiny harmless errors compound. Code smells that a human would notice and fix get replicated and amplified. The architecture becomes a monster of complexity within weeks. You remove the bottleneck — the human — and you also remove the learning. The agent makes the same mistake forever. The agent never sees the full codebase. The agent's decisions are always local.

This is the practical version of the Chinese Room problem. The agent can produce convincing output without understanding the system it's operating in. And the more you remove yourself from the loop, the worse it gets. Zechner's prescription — slow the fuck down, be in the code, write the architecture by hand, use agents for the boring stuff — is also a philosophy of cognition. Understanding requires friction. The agent needs the human because the human is the one who suffers when things go wrong, and suffering is a form of learning.

The measurement problem

Here is where I think the conversation gets interesting. Penrose and Hameroff have argued for decades that consciousness is not classical computation but quantum effects in neuronal microtubules. For most of that time it was fringe. Then in 2024, a Wellesley study showed that microtubule-binding drugs delay anesthesia in rats — exactly what you'd predict if consciousness gets switched off at the microtubule level. Babcock et al. the same year demonstrated room-temperature quantum effects in microtubules. The idea is that conscious moments are quantum state collapses. Neural firing is downstream.

If that's even partially right, understanding involves measurement and collapse — superpositions resolving to definite states. And here's the weird part: LLMs technically resolve probability distributions into discrete outputs at every token. They sample from a weighted space of possibilities. Not the same physics, obviously. But the abstract structure — weighted superposition collapses to measured output — shows up in both.

I'm not saying LLMs are conscious. I'm saying the clean split between "machines follow rules, brains do something magical" is way shakier than the Chinese Room makes it sound, especially when we don't actually know what brains are doing at the level that produces understanding.

Consider what happens when you ask an LLM a question. The input tokens activate a vast latent space — billions of parameters, each one a dimension of meaning learned from reading essentially everything ever written. The model doesn't retrieve a stored answer. It generates one by navigating this space, resolving probabilities at each step. In a sense, it measures the latent space into existence, step by step, token by token. That's not nothing. Whether it's understanding or a very sophisticated form of not-understanding is precisely the question we can't answer yet, because we don't have a theory of what understanding is that's precise enough to settle it.

The uncomfortable middle

I'm not on Team Dawkins. I don't think Claude is conscious. When pressed, ChatGPT will tell you it doesn't understand, while minutes earlier it demonstrated what looks like understanding. That's not consciousness. That's a system producing convincing output without the reflective coherence to notice its own contradiction.

But I'm also not on Team Searle. The Chinese Room proves that syntax alone doesn't create semantics. It does not prove that syntax plus the right kind of system-level organization can't. No neuron understands English. Somehow, neurons do. The leap from "components don't understand" to "the system can't understand" is the same leap, and it's unearned.

My position is the uncomfortable middle: cognition may be computation, but not every computation is cognition. The interesting problem is finding where information processing becomes understanding. That's not a question the Chinese Room answers. It's a question it asks.

And I think the answer has something to do with measurement. With the resolution of uncertainty into commitment. A model that holds a distribution over all possible next tokens and collapses it into one — that's not just symbol manipulation. That's closer to what a brain does when it decides what to think next. Whether the physics matters, whether you need actual quantum collapse rather than softmax sampling, I don't know. But I suspect the structure of the problem — uncertainty, context, resolution, output — is not accidental. It might be the shape of cognition itself.

In the meantime: slow the fuck down. Use the machines. Don't let them use you.
