OK Computer

Radiohead 1997 9.5 / 10

Quick Take

The album that proved rock music could be as intellectually ambitious as classical or literature — without losing its visceral punch.

Review

Before OK Computer, "alternative rock" mostly meant loud guitars and adolescent angst. Radiohead's third album asked: what if rock music took the anxieties of late capitalism seriously — the alienation, the information overload, the feeling that technology was accelerating beyond human control — and turned them into something beautiful?

The result is an album that operates on multiple levels. On the surface, a collection of impeccably crafted rock songs. Beneath that, a cohesive artistic statement about modernity. And beneath that, a series of sonic experiments that expand what a guitar band can sound like — the Mellotron textures of "Airbag," the distorted chaos of "Climbing Up the Walls," the cathedral-like architecture of "Let Down."

Thom Yorke's voice has never been more expressive — shifting from whispered vulnerability to full-throated desperation within the same phrase. Jonny Greenwood's arrangements reveal a musician thinking in compositional terms far beyond rock convention.

Context

Recorded in 1996 at St. Catherine's Court, a manor house near Bath, England. Produced by Nigel Godrich, who would become Radiohead's longtime collaborator. Released June 1997 to critical acclaim but modest initial commercial expectations. It went on to define "art rock" for the next two decades and is consistently ranked among the greatest albums ever made.

Key Tracks

  • Paranoid Android — A six-minute epic in four movements. Bohemian Rhapsody for the Prozac generation.
  • Let Down — The most beautiful song about disappointment ever written.
  • Karma Police — A deceptively simple piano ballad that builds into something menacing.
  • No Surprises — A lullaby sung by someone who has given up. The glockenspiel is genius.
  • Lucky — The emotional center of the album.
